1 Folding of RNA Molecules
Discoveries in recent decades have established a wide range of biological roles served by
RNA molecules, in addition to their better-known role as carriers of the coded messages
that direct ribosomes to construct specic proteins. Non-coding RNA molecules participate
in gene regulation, DNA and RNA repair, splicing and self-splicing, catalysis, protein
synthesis, and intracellular transportation. For biological macromolecules, structure is
function. Experimental determination of RNA structures is still time-consuming, and
computational approaches are of great importance. The prevailing computational approach
to the determination of RNA secondary structures is through the thermal equilibrium, via
Monte Carlo sampling or the approximation of the minimum free energy conguration.
However, the biological relevance of equilibrium congurations is the subject of enduring
debate.
In [6], we adopt a kinetic perspective, and carry out statistical analyses on RNA primary
and secondary structure data (sequence and base-pairs data), which suggest an intra-
molecular statistical relationship between secondary and primary structures as an essential
characteristic for the existence of reliable folding on biologically relevant time scales. These
experiments, the rst of their kind, are simple in that they involve only RNA primary and
secondary structure data and elementary counting statistics, yet they yield signicant
insight into the folding of non-coding RNA molecules. Notably, they are consistent with
folding to metastable but not necessarily equilibrium structures.

3 Methodological Research
3.1 Overcoming entropic barriers in stochastic dynamics
Stochastic methods play an important role in the study of kinetics. Oftentimes, the e-
ciency of stochastic approaches to molecular dynamics critically depends upon the eciency
of reaching small targets in a vast landscape of possible congurations. This search is chal-
lenged by what is, eectively, an entropic barrier. Experimental evidence suggests that
such entropic barriers are ubiquitous in biomolecular systems, and often characterize the
rate-limiting step of biomolecular processes, such as the folding of RNA molecules.
In [2], we develop a theoretical understanding of entropic barriers from the perspective of
hitting probabilities for small targets, thereby providing valuable kinetic information. This
is achieved by establishing the near invariance of hitting probabilities to initial conditions
away from the targets. Given this invariance, a further invariance of hitting probabilities
to the energy landscape away from the targets can be established. This further invari-
ance points to ways to accurately approximate the hitting probabilities using only local
2
information around the targets. It turns out the local information comes in the form of
capacities of local sets around the targets. Using a new capacity estimation algorithm,
we have developed Capacity Hopping (CHop), which is an ecient approximation for
simulating a sequence of hitting events. Numerical experiments on a prototypical entropic
barrier, a toy model with a golf-course like potential, show that CHop is nearly as accurate
as direct simulations in estimating hitting probabilities, but over six-hundred times faster.
The issue of entropic barriers has implications for general frameworks like kinetic transition
networks and Markov state models. Moving forward, an important part of my research
plan is to study these implications and to elucidate the connections. Furthermore, I see
this work as laying the foundation for a general-purpose method for the folding of RNA
molecules. The idea is to formulate the folding problem as one of exploring a hierarchy of
golf courses: each part of the conguration space that is consistent with a particular sec-
ondary structure denes its own golf course; the holes in this course are other secondary
structures obtained by forming new base pairs; breaking base pairs would bring us back to
a higher-level golf course, while forming base pairs would bring us into a more rened,
lower-level, course. CHop would serve as a fundamental tool in this picture. This is an
ambitious proposal, and opens many interesting questions involving the geometry of the
conguration space and the folding dynamics.
3.2 Multiscale methods for stochastic optimization
A dierent kind of stochastic dynamics is given by stochastic gradient descent (SGD), which
has emerged as an eective way to optimize large-scale nonlinear systems. In applying
SGD, two common problems arise:
1. Ill-conditioning: sometimes also referred to as pathological curvature. It appears when
we have regions in which the loss functions are steep in certain directions but at in
others. When ill-conditioning is present, optimization becomes dicult since we are
forced to make small steps in the at directions, in order to avoid explosion in the
steep directions.
2. Noisy gradients estimates: it appears because in SGD, we evaluate gradients using
only a subset of the data. When the noise-to-signal ratio becomes too high, opti-
mization becomes dicult since the estimated gradients contain very little valuable
information.
These problems motivate the development of many popular methods, e.g. momentum,
Hessian-free optimization and stochastic variance reduced gradient. In [4], we adapt ideas
from multigrid methods, and study these problems in the context of training deep neural
networks. Multigrid methods were originally developed for the ecient solution of dieren-
tial equations. They have proved to be a powerful tool to solve the ill-conditioning problem.
In [4], we make use of the characteristics of the neural network architectures and the SGD
training process, and design ecient coarsening and interpolation procedures to construct
suitable coarse-level gradients for the application of multigrid methods. Empirical results
show good performance in addressing both problems.

3.3 Gradient-based MCMC for Discrete Random Variables
Gradient-based MCMC methods, including Langevin dynamics and Hamiltonian Monte
Carlo, are widely used and often spectacularly successful. They are supported by con-
venient and highly optimized software packages. However, there are obvious barriers to
applying these methods to discrete systems. In addition to addressing the computational
issues for our compositional models, the novel embedding proposed in [5] provides a general
framework for designing gradient-based MCMC methods in probabilistic models with dis-
crete random variables. I am currently working on implementations of these embeddings
and studying the many theoretical questions posed by the general approach.
4 Bridging Physical Sciences with Machine Learning
4.1 Stochastic Dynamics in Machine Learning
Gradient-based methods play an increasingly important role in both inference and learn-
ing for machine learning models. This is largely due to the availability of powerful au-
tomatic dierentiation packages. Representative gradient-based methods for inference in-
clude Langevin dynamics, Hamiltonian Monte Carlo, and SGD applied under the vari-
ational inference framework; representative gradient-based methods for learning usually
involve applying SGD to loss minimization (or likelihood maximization when a probabilis-
tic model is present). All of these gradient-based methods induce some kind of stochastic
dynamics. This makes stochastic dynamics, which has been studied in great depth in the
physical literature, an important topic in machine learning.
In studying the folding of RNA molecules, exposure to the physical literature introduced
me to a variety of advanced methods for the study of stochastic dynamics. These meth-
ods contain a large number of valuable ideas that can inform both inference and learning.
Methods from the thermodynamic perspective provide information about equilibrium dis-
tributions, while methods from the kinetic perspective provide insights into the stochastic
optimization process. Beyond plans that stem directly from my current and ongoing re-
search projects, I will study the feasibility of exploiting these methods in machine learning.
My initial focus will be on developing a coarse-grained representation in order to better
understand the equilibrium distribution (for inference purposes) and the kinetics (for learn-
ing purposes). Relevant methods in the physical literature include Markov state models
for constructing the coarse-grained representation, and transition path/interface sampling
and transition path theory for illuminating the kinetics of transitions among meta-stable
states.

is the emerging marriage of statistics and dynamical systems. Representative challenges,
on which there has been much recent progress, include data-driven discovery of ordinary
and partial dierential equations, new approaches to system identication using tools from
